## Key Findings from Search Research

### Alaska (§ 45.48.010)
- Disclosure must be made in the most expeditious time possible without unreasonable delay
- No disclosure required if, after investigation, determined there is no reasonable likelihood of harm
- Determination must be documented in writing and maintained for 5 years
- Attorney general must be notified in writing

### Arizona (§ 18-552)
- Investigation must be conducted promptly to determine if breach occurred
- Notification required within 45 days after determination of breach
- Must notify attorney general if breach affects 1,000+ individuals
- Must notify three largest nationwide consumer reporting agencies

### Maine (Title 10, § 1346 et seq.)
- Notification required no more than 30 days after awareness of breach
- Must conduct good faith investigation to determine likelihood of misuse
- Must notify appropriate state regulators or Attorney General

### New Hampshire (§ 359-C:19 et seq.)
- § 359-C:19 provides definitions (personal information, security breach)
- § 359-C:20 requires notification as soon as possible if misuse has occurred or is reasonably likely

### Rhode Island (§ 11-49.3-1 et seq.)
- Identity Theft Protection Act of 2015
- Municipal/state agencies: notification within 30 calendar days
- Other persons: notification within 45 calendar days
- Civil penalty: up to $200 per record for knowing and willful violations

### Wisconsin (§ 134.98)
- Applies to entities with principal place of business in Wisconsin OR maintaining/licensing PI in Wisconsin
- No notice required if acquisition does not create material risk of identity theft/fraud
- Must notify 3 largest consumer reporting agencies if 1,000+ individuals affected
